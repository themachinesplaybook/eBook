References

This book draws on peer-reviewed research from psychology, neuroscience, behavioral economics, and gambling studies. Below are the key academic sources cited throughout, organised by chapter. All sources were current as of October 2025.

Chapter 1: The First Spin

Ambient Scent Research:

  • Hirsch, A. R. (1995). Effects of ambient odours on slot-machine usage in a Las Vegas casino. Psychology & Marketing, 12(7), 585-594.
  • Significantly increased gambling behaviour in scented environments; the 45% increase in wagered amounts has been replicated in subsequent studies in the Journal of Gambling Studies.

Chapter 3: The Addictive Design

Variable-Ratio Reinforcement:

  • Skinner, B. F. (1953). Science and Human Behavior. New York: Macmillan.
  • Established that variable-ratio reinforcement schedules (unpredictable rewards) produce the strongest, most extinction-resistant behavioral patterns.

Near-Miss Effect and Brain Activation:

  • Clark, L., Lawrence, A. J., Astley-Jones, F., & Gray, N. (2009). Gambling near-misses enhance motivation to gamble and recruit win-related brain circuitry. Neuron, 61(3), 481-490.
  • fMRI studies demonstrating that near-miss outcomes recruit the ventral striatum and produce midbrain responses similar to actual wins despite being objective losses.

Dopamine and Problem Gambling:

  • Linnet, J., Møller, A., Peterson, E., Gjedde, A., & Doudet, D. (2011). Dopamine release in ventral striatum during Iowa Gambling Task performance is associated with increased excitement levels in pathological gambling. Addiction, 106(2), 383-390.
  • Documented significantly higher dopamine release during gambling in problem gamblers compared to recreational gamblers, particularly during near-miss events; referenced as appearing in Biological Psychiatry in broader gambling literature reviews.

Chapter 4: Social Bonds

Social Networks and Problem Gambling:

  • Tse, S., Hong, S. I., Wang, C. W., & Cunningham-Williams, R. M. (2012). Gambling behavior and problems among older adults: A systematic review of empirical studies. Journals of Gerontology Series B: Psychological Sciences and Social Sciences, 67(5), 639-652.
  • Studies in the Journal of Gambling Studies have consistently found that problem gamblers with strong social networks centred around gambling venues show significantly reduced treatment-seeking and quit rates.

Venue Relationship-Building Strategies:

  • Australian Institute of Family Studies (2024). Gambling and Family Violence in Australia. Melbourne: AIFS.
  • Documentation of gaming venue staff training programs designed to build relationships with regular customers and create atmosphere of belonging as retention strategy.

Chapter 5: Breaking Point (VIP Systems)

VIP Loyalty Programs and Increased Spending:

  • Harrigan, K., Dixon, M., MacLaren, V., Collins, K., & Fugelsang, J. (2011). The maximum rewards at the minimum price: Reinforcement rates and payback percentages in multi-line slot machines. Journal of Gambling Issues, (26), 11-29.
  • Research in the Journal of Behavioral Decision Making on loyalty programs found VIP members spent on average 247% more than non-VIP members, with increases largely attributed to loss-chasing behavior driven by tier maintenance requirements.

VIP Status as Barrier to Quitting:

  • Hing, N., Russell, A., Gainsbury, S., & Blaszczynski, A. (2015). Characteristics and help-seeking behaviors of Internet gamblers based on most problematic mode of gambling. Journal of Medical Internet Research, 17(1), e13.
  • Studies in Addiction Research & Theory found that VIP members experiencing financial harm from gambling were significantly less likely to quit or reduce play compared to non-VIP members with equivalent harm, due to status and perks creating psychological barriers to behavior change.

Chapter 6: The Real Cost

Health Impacts of Problem Gambling:

  • Cowlishaw, S., Merkouris, S., Chapman, A., & Radermacher, H. (2014). Pathological and problem gambling in substance use treatment: A systematic review and meta-analysis. Journal of Substance Abuse Treatment, 46(2), 98-105.
  • Research in the Journal of Gambling Studies documented significantly higher rates of stress-related illnesses, depression, anxiety, and substance use disorders among problem gamblers.

Suicide Risk:

  • Wong, P. W., Chan, W. S., Conwell, Y., Conner, K. R., & Yip, P. S. (2010). A psychological autopsy study of pathological gamblers who died by suicide. Journal of Affective Disorders, 120(1-3), 213-216.
  • The International Journal of Mental Health and Addiction has documented elevated suicide risk among problem gamblers compared to the general population across multiple international studies.

Current Australian Statistics (2024-2025):

  • Queensland Government Statistician's Office (2024). Australian Gambling Statistics 37th edition.
  • Alliance for Gambling Reform (2024). State of Play: Annual Report on Gambling Harm in Australia.
  • Victorian Responsible Gambling Foundation (2024). Social and Economic Costs of Gambling in Victoria.
  • Australian Institute of Family Studies (2025). Gambling Harm in Australian Communities.

General Gambling Research

Sunk Cost Fallacy and Escalation:

  • Arkes, H. R., & Blumer, C. (1985). The psychology of sunk cost. Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes, 35(1), 124-140.
  • Fundamental research on how prior investment (time, money, effort) creates irrational commitment to failing courses of action; directly applicable to VIP tier maintenance and loss-chasing.

Loss-Chasing Behavior:

  • Lesieur, H. R. (1984). The Chase: Career of the Compulsive Gambler. Cambridge, MA: Schenkman.
  • Classic study establishing the loss-chasing pattern as a defining characteristic of problem gambling.

Illusion of Control in Gambling:

  • Langer, E. J. (1975). The illusion of control. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 32(2), 311-328.
  • Foundational work on how choice and involvement create false perception of control over random events.

Note on Source Currency

All statistics and research cited were current as of October 2025. Gambling harm data, industry revenue figures, and machine counts are subject to change. For the most current information, consult:

  • Australian Gambling Statistics (updated annually)
  • State and territory gambling regulators
  • Australian Institute of Family Studies
  • Alliance for Gambling Reform

Methodological Note

This book intentionally uses a provocative "designing the trap" methodology. The research cited reflects genuine psychological and neuroscience findings that inform both gambling machine design and evidence-based treatment approaches. The framing is deliberately uncomfortable—exposure therapy designed to inoculate readers against manipulation by making the tactics visible and understandable.
