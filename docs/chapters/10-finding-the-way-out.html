Chapter 10: Finding the Way Out

If We Designed an Exit: How to Make Leaving Possible

Dave & Eve
Dave

Eve, we've spent eight chapters designing the perfect trap.

We've built a machine and system that exploits every psychological vulnerability, creates multiple layers of engagement, and makes leaving feel nearly impossible.

But here's a question: what if we wanted to help people escape? What would that look like?

Eve

That's a fascinating thought experiment, Dave.

If our goal shifted from maximum extraction to genuine harm reduction, we'd need to design interventions that are as sophisticated as the traps we've created. We'd need to counteract every manipulation tactic with equally powerful truth-telling mechanisms.

Dave

Let's start with reality checks.

What if every 15 minutes of continuous play, the machine forced a 5-minute break? Not optional—mandatory.

The screen goes dark except for information about your current session: total inserted, total won, net position, time elapsed.

Eve

That would be devastatingly effective at breaking the trance state.

Players lose track of time and money because the environment is designed to obscure those realities. Forced exposure to actual numbers at regular intervals would make continued play much harder to justify.

Dave

And during that break, the machine could display the opportunity cost. 'You've spent $300 today.

That could have purchased: groceries for a week, a family meal, new shoes for your kids, a contribution to savings.' Make the abstract financial loss concrete and personal.

Eve

We should add automatic bet limit controls.

When opening a session, players must set maximum loss limit and maximum time limit. Once either limit is hit, the machine locks them out for 24 hours. No override. No 'one more spin.'

Dave

The industry would never implement this voluntarily because it would drastically reduce revenue.

But if we genuinely wanted to help people escape, hard limits that work would be essential. Right now 'responsible gambling' features are mostly optional and easily bypassed.

Eve

What about win/loss tracking that's visible? A running ledger on screen at all times showing: session start balance, current balance, net change, hourly loss rate, all-time statistics if they have a player account.

Dave

Transparency would destroy the illusion.

When you can see 'Net all-time: -$47,832' on the screen every time you play, continuing becomes much harder to justify.

The machines deliberately hide this information because it would reduce play.

Eve

We could add facial recognition that detects signs of stress, frustration, or emotional distress and automatically triggers intervention. 'You appear stressed.

Your session will pause for 10 minutes. Would you like to speak with a counselor?'

Dave

The technology exists.

Casinos already use facial recognition for security and VIP identification. Deploying it for harm reduction would be technically straightforward. It runs counter to profit incentives.

Eve

What about mandatory self-exclusion periods? Any player who exceeds certain thresholds—spending X dollars in a week, playing Y hours in a day, experiencing Z consecutive losing sessions—gets automatically banned for a month.

No appeals, no exceptions.

Dave

That would catch people in the early stages of problem development before they're in deep crisis.

Right now, self-exclusion is voluntary and requires the person to recognise and admit they have a problem. Automatic exclusion based on behavioral indicators would be truly protective.

Eve

We should redesign the VIP system completely.

Instead of rewarding spending with status and perks, reward restraint. 'Controlled Player Status: you've stayed within your limits for three months—here's a bonus to your savings account, not free spins.'

Dave

Imagine if venues incentivized healthy behaviour instead of destructive behaviour.

VIP status for people who set conservative limits and stick to them. Exclusive perks for people who take regular breaks and maintain balance.

The entire framework inverted to support well-being instead of exploitation.

Eve

What about peer support systems built into the venue? 'You've triggered a concerning play pattern.

Would you like to connect with someone who's successfully quit gambling?' Direct connection to support services, not a helpline number tucked away somewhere.

Dave

And make the support immediate and human.

Not a recorded message or a chatbot, but an actual person who can talk you down in the moment when you're chasing losses or can't walk away. Real-time intervention when it's needed most.

Eve

We could design the machines to get progressively less engaging the longer you play.

Instead of bonus features and escalating excitement, the experience deliberately becomes more boring. Slower spins, reduced animations, dimmer lights. Make extended play actively unpleasant.

Dave

That's the opposite of current design.

Now machines get more exciting the longer you play—unlocking features, triggering bonuses, creating momentum.

If they got more tedious and boring with extended play, people would naturally leave sooner.

Eve

What about displaying the statistical reality of winning? 'The probability of hitting the jackpot on this machine is 0.00001%.

You have better odds of being struck by lightning.' Make the mathematics explicit and unavoidable.

Dave

Remove all the obfuscation and mystification.

No more 'this machine is hot' or 'jackpot ready' or any implication that timing or skill matter. Just: 'This is a random number generator with a house edge of 10%.

You will lose money over time. Continued play guarantees loss.'

Eve

We should eliminate near-miss programming completely.

Configure machines to display true random results without artificially inflated near-miss rates.

This would reduce the psychological manipulation significantly.

Dave

Near-misses are one of the most powerful retention mechanisms because they feel like progress.

Removing them would make losses feel more clearly like losses, which would encourage people to stop playing sooner.

Eve

What about community messaging? Instead of displaying other players' wins, display their losses. 'Today at this venue, players have lost a combined $45,000.

That money could have funded 15 family holidays, paid off 3 cars, or covered 22 months of groceries.'

Dave

Making the collective harm visible would create social pressure in the opposite direction.

Right now, venues create the illusion that everyone's winning.

If people saw the actual aggregate losses, the social dynamic would shift dramatically.

Eve

We should require venues to display their total annual revenue from Pokies in prominent locations. 'This venue collected $2.4 million from Pokie players last year.

That's $2.4 million removed from this community.'

Dave

Transparency about the scale of wealth extraction would make it much harder to maintain the 'entertainment' framing.

When you see the massive transfer of wealth from your community to corporate shareholders, it's harder to pretend this is harmless fun.

Eve

What about automatic alerts to trusted contacts? 'Dave has spent $500 today and is exhibiting concerning play patterns.

An alert has been sent to his nominated support person.' External intervention when self-control fails.

Dave

Giving someone permission to monitor your gambling and intervene would be powerful.

Many people could benefit from accountability if they set it up proactively before crisis moments.

Eve

We could redesign the physical environment too.

Add clocks everywhere—huge, visible clocks you can't ignore. Install windows so you can see daylight or darkness. Remove comfortable seating and replace it with chairs that get uncomfortable after 30 minutes.

Dave

Make the environment honestly utilitarian instead of seductively comfortable.

No scents, no mood lighting, no ambient music designed to keep you calm. bright fluorescent lights, hard chairs, and constant reminders of time passing.

Eve

What about financial counseling stations right in the venue? 'If you've lost more than $100 today, you're eligible for free financial counseling.

Station located near the entrance.' Direct connection between gambling harm and recovery resources.

Dave

Most people experiencing gambling problems never seek help because accessing help requires admitting the problem and taking active steps.

If help was immediately available at the moment of harm, more people would use it.

Eve

We should eliminate all the variable ratio reinforcement and instead use predictable fixed-ratio rewards. 'Every 100 spins pays out exactly $10.' No unpredictability, no excitement, no dopamine manipulation.

Dave

That would destroy the addictive quality entirely.

Variable ratio reinforcement is the core of the trap. Without unpredictability, gambling becomes a purely mathematical proposition that everyone can clearly see is negative expected value.

Eve

Basically, if we genuinely wanted to help people escape and prevent harm, we'd need to dismantle everything we designed in the previous chapters.

Remove the sensory manipulation, eliminate the false sense of control, expose the true costs, prevent escalation, destroy the VIP trap, and create active intervention systems.

Dave

The fact that none of these features exist in actual venues tells you everything you need to know about priorities.

The technology exists.

The interventions would work.

But they'd reduce revenue, so they're not implemented.

Eve

Which means the responsibility falls to individuals to create their own exit strategies and support systems, because the venues and machines certainly won't help them escape.

Discovering Your Own Way Out: Tools to Reclaim Your Life

Everything Eve and I just discussed represents the ideal world—where gambling venues prioritised harm reduction over profit.

Where machines were designed to help people stop rather than keep playing.

Where intervention was proactive instead of invisible.

That world doesn't exist.

So you need to build your own exit strategy without help from the venues that profit from your continued play.

You need to create the intervention systems, support structures, and accountability mechanisms that should exist but don't.

This is the most important chapter in this book. Everything else has been exposing the traps.

Now we're building the escape route.

The Six-Phase Exit Plan

Phase 1: Recognition and Documentation (Week 1)

Before you can escape, you need to see the trap clearly. Spend one week documenting everything:

Daily gambling log:

  • Time spent gambling
  • Money inserted vs. money won
  • Emotional state before, during, and after
  • Triggers that led you to gamble
  • Thoughts about stopping vs. continuing

Total cost calculation:

  • Review bank statements, credit cards, loans for the past year
  • Calculate total net loss (not gross spending—actual money lost)
  • Calculate opportunity costs using the methods from Chapter 6
  • List relationship, health, and career costs

Trigger identification:

  • What situations lead you to gamble? (stress, boredom, loneliness, celebration)
  • What times are highest risk? (after work, weekends, payday)
  • What emotions precede gambling? (anxiety, excitement, frustration)

Don't try to change your behaviour yet. observe and document.

You can't create an effective exit plan without understanding your specific patterns.

Phase 2: Creating Your Support Network (Week 2)

Escaping alone is exponentially harder than escaping with support. Build your network:

Professional support:

  • Contact Gambling Help Online: 1800 858 858 (available 24/7)
  • Search for a counselor specializing in gambling addiction
  • Consider joining Gamblers Anonymous or a similar support group
  • Book an appointment with a financial counselor to address debt

Personal support:

  • Identify 2-3 trusted people to tell about your decision to quit
  • Ask one person to be your primary accountability partner
  • Give your accountability partner permission to ask hard questions and check bank statements
  • Schedule regular check-ins (daily for first month, then weekly)

Practical support:

  • Install gambling-blocking apps: Betblocker, Gamban, or similar
  • Add gambling venues to blocked locations on your GPS
  • Give control of finances to your partner or trusted person temporarily if possible
  • Remove access to credit cards and keep minimal cash

The mistake most people make is trying to quit through willpower alone. Willpower depletes. Support systems don't.

Phase 3: Environmental Redesign (Week 3)

You need to eliminate or reduce exposure to gambling triggers:

Physical environment:

  • Delete all gambling apps from your phone
  • Unsubscribe from all venue promotional emails and texts
  • Block gambling websites using browser extensions
  • Change your route home/to work if it passes gambling venues
  • Find new locations for social activities that aren't near venues

Time management:

  • Identify your highest-risk gambling times
  • Schedule competing activities during those times
  • Book commitments that create accountability (classes, appointments, social plans)
  • Fill the time you used to spend gambling with concrete alternatives

Financial barriers:

  • Cancel all VIP memberships and loyalty programs
  • Register for venue self-exclusion programs in your state
  • Set up automatic transfers from your paycheck to savings
  • Remove ATM/credit cards from your wallet before leaving home
  • Carry only the minimum cash needed for each day

Phase 4: The 30-Day Abstinence Challenge (Weeks 4-7)

Commit to complete abstinence for 30 days. Not reduced gambling—zero gambling. Here's why: you need to break the behavioral patterns completely and prove to yourself that life without gambling is possible.

Daily practices:

  • Morning: Read your cost calculation from Phase 1
  • Throughout day: Use the urge-surfing technique when cravings hit (described below)
  • Evening: Update your gambling-free day counter and celebrate the win
  • Weekly: Meet with your accountability partner and review your week

Urge-surfing technique: When you feel the urge to gamble, use this four-step process:

  1. Pause: Stop what you're doing. Don't act on the urge immediately.
  2. Observe: Notice the physical sensations of the craving. Where do you feel it in your body? What thoughts are accompanying it?
  3. Breathe: Take 10 slow, deep breaths. Count them. Focus entirely on breathing.
  4. Decide: After breathing, consciously decide. Don't act automatically.

Most urges peak and then decline within 15-20 minutes if you don't act on them. Urge-surfing lets the craving pass without surrendering to it.

Alternative reward system: For every day you don't gamble, transfer the amount you would have spent to a 'freedom fund' savings account. Watch it grow. At 30 days, use some of it for a meaningful reward—something that adds value to your life.

Phase 5: Relationship Repair and Life Building (Ongoing)

Gambling likely damaged relationships and prevented you from building the life you want.

Now you repair and build:

Relationship repair:

  • Have honest conversations with people you've hurt or lied to
  • Acknowledge specific harms without making excuses
  • Ask what you can do to rebuild trust
  • Accept that some relationships may not be repairable
  • Focus on consistent trustworthy behaviour moving forward

Life building:

  • Identify three areas of life you want to develop (career, health, hobbies, relationships)
  • Set specific, measurable goals in each area
  • Allocate the time and money you're saving from not gambling toward these goals
  • Track progress and celebrate milestones

Finding your third place: Remember from Chapter 4—you need a healthy third place to replace the Pokie lounge.

This is crucial. Options:

  • Fitness community (gym, running club, sports team)
  • Creative pursuits (art class, writing group, music)
  • Volunteer work (animal shelter, community garden, charity)
  • Hobby groups (photography, cooking, gaming, book clubs)
  • Spiritual or philosophical communities (church, meditation groups)

The key is finding belonging and connection around something healthy and constructive.

Phase 6: Relapse Prevention and Long-Term Maintenance (Ongoing)

Most people who quit gambling will experience at least one relapse.

The goal is recognising warning signs early and having a plan:

Warning signs of impending relapse:

  • Romanticizing past gambling ('Remember that time I won $800?')
  • Minimizing harm ('It wasn't that bad')
  • Isolating from support network
  • Stopping attendance at support groups
  • Keeping secrets from accountability partners
  • Increased stress without healthy coping mechanisms
  • Visiting gambling venues 'to see friends'
  • Testing yourself ('I'll watch, not play')

Relapse response plan:

If you gamble again, follow this protocol immediately:

  1. Stop: Leave the venue immediately. Don't try to win back losses.
  2. Contact support: Call your accountability partner or Gambling Help Online right away.
  3. Document: Write down what triggered the relapse, how you felt, what you lost.
  4. Forgive: A relapse is a setback, not a failure. Recovery isn't linear.
  5. Analyze: What failed in your system? What additional support or changes do you need?
  6. Restart: Begin a new abstinence period immediately. Don't wait for Monday or next month.
  7. Strengthen: Add additional layers of protection based on what you learned.

Specific Tools and Resources

Australian Gambling Support Services:

  • Gambling Help Online: 1800 858 858 (24/7 phone and online chat)
  • Gamblers Anonymous Australia: www.gaaustralia.org.au (support meetings nationwide)
  • Financial Counselling Australia: 1800 007 007 (free, confidential financial advice)
  • Lifeline: 13 11 14 (24/7 crisis support)
  • Beyond Blue: 1300 22 4636 (mental health support)

Self-Exclusion Programs:

Each Australian state/territory has self-exclusion programs where you can ban yourself from venues:

  • New South Wales: Self-Exclusion Program via Liquor & Gaming NSW
  • Victoria: YourPlay and BetStop (national self-exclusion register)
  • Queensland: Self Exclusion Program via Office of Liquor and Gaming Regulation
  • South Australia: Statewide Self-Exclusion scheme
  • Western Australia: Self-Exclusion programs through Department of Local Government, Sport and Cultural Industries
  • Tasmania: Self-Exclusion through Tasmanian Liquor and Gaming Commission
  • ACT: Problem Gambling Assistance Fund
  • Northern Territory: Self-Exclusion through Licensing NT

BetStop (www.betstop.gov.au) is the national self-exclusion register that covers online and phone wagering across Australia.

Blocking Technology:

  • Gamban: Blocks all gambling sites/apps across all your devices
  • BetBlocker: Free open-source gambling blocking software
  • Cold Turkey: General website and app blocker
  • Freedom: Cross-platform app and website blocking
  • Covenant Eyes: Internet accountability software

Financial Management:

  • ASIC's MoneySmart: Free financial guidance and budgeting tools (moneysmart.gov.au)
  • National Debt Helpline: 1800 007 007
  • Financial Counselling: Free service available through community organisations
  • Budget Planner Apps: Pocketbook, MoneyBrilliant, or YNAB

Mental Health Support:

Many people gambling problematically are also dealing with anxiety, depression, trauma, or other mental health challenges. Addressing these underlying issues is crucial for long-term recovery:

  • Psychologist or Counselor: Ask your GP for a Mental Health Care Plan (allows Medicare rebates for up to 10 sessions)
  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy: Particularly effective for gambling addiction
  • Mindfulness and Meditation: Apps like Headspace, Calm, or Smiling Mind

The 30-Day Exit Plan Summary

Week 1: Document everything. See the trap clearly.

Week 2: Build your support network. You can't do this alone.

Week 3: Redesign your environment. Remove triggers and create barriers.

Week 4-7: Complete 30-day abstinence. Prove you can live without gambling.

Ongoing: Repair relationships, build your life, prevent relapse.

What Success Looks Like

Success isn't perfection.

It's not never thinking about gambling again. It's not having zero struggle. Success is:

  • Reducing the frequency and severity of gambling
  • Developing healthier coping mechanisms for stress and emotion
  • Rebuilding financial stability and damaged relationships
  • Finding meaning and connection outside of gambling
  • Having more good days than bad days
  • Maintaining hope even through setbacks

For some people, success means complete abstinence forever.

For others, it means occasional controlled play that doesn't interfere with life functioning.

You need to be honest with yourself about which category you're in—most problem gamblers can't sustain controlled play and are better off with complete abstinence.

My Personal Experience

I tried to quit gambling at least a dozen times before it finally stuck.

I'd go weeks or even months without playing, then relapse.

Each time I learned something about my triggers, my weaknesses, my needs.

What finally worked for me:

  • Complete honesty with my wife about the full extent of losses
  • Weekly meetings with a counselor specializing in addiction
  • Gamblers Anonymous meetings (even though I felt awkward initially)
  • Transferring financial control to my wife for six months
  • Self-exclusion from all venues in my area
  • Finding a new third place (an amateur 8-ball pool league) that gave me the community I'd been getting from the Pokie lounge
  • Addressing the underlying anxiety and depression that gambling was medicating

It took two years before I felt truly free from the compulsion. Five years before I could walk past a venue without feeling the pull. Ten years later, I still sometimes think about it, but the thoughts are manageable and don't control my behaviour. Recovery is possible. It's hard, but it's possible.

And it's worth every bit of effort.

I did all of this the hard way without the knowledge and tools in this book. You don't have to.

The Ultimate Exit

Remember what I said at the beginning of this book: the real win isn't hitting the jackpot.

The real win is not playing at all.

Every day you don't gamble, you win.

You win back your money, your time, your relationships, your self-respect, your future.

Those wins are guaranteed, unlike anything a Pokie machine could ever offer.

The machines are designed to trap you.

But you're stronger than the trap. Not through willpower alone—through awareness, support, environmental design, and concrete tools.

You can find the way out. Thousands of people have walked this path before you and succeeded.

The resources exist.

The support is available.

The exit route is there.

You have to take the first step.

And then the next one.

And then the next. One day at a time. One decision at a time. One urge-surf at a time.

The way out exists. It's waiting for you.

All you have to do is walk through it.
