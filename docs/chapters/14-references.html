This book draws on extensive research from psychology, neuroscience, gambling studies, and public health. The following sources inform the analysis of poker machine design and addiction mechanisms.

Research Citations by Chapter

Chapter 1: The Venue as Trap

Scent Marketing Research:

  • Hirsch, A. R. (1995). "Effects of ambient odors on slot-machine usage in a Las Vegas casino." Psychology & Marketing, 12(7), 585-594.
  • Research demonstrates that pleasant ambient scents can increase gambling expenditure by up to 45% compared to unscented environments.

Chapter 2: The Machine's Psychological Design

Operant Conditioning and Variable-Ratio Reinforcement:

  • Skinner, B. F. (1953). Science and Human Behavior. New York: Macmillan.
  • Skinner, B. F. (1957). "Schedules of Reinforcement." Psychological Review.
  • Variable-ratio reinforcement schedules produce the most persistent behaviour patterns, highly resistant to extinction.

Near-Miss Programming and Neuroscience:

  • Clark, L., et al. (2009). "Gambling near-misses enhance motivation to gamble and recruit win-related brain circuitry." Neuron, 61(3), 481-490.
  • Chase, H. W., & Clark, L. (2010). "Gambling severity predicts midbrain response to near-miss outcomes." Journal of Neuroscience, 30(18), 6180-6187.
  • fMRI studies show near-misses activate the striatum (reward centre) similarly to actual wins, despite representing losses.

Dopamine and Problem Gambling:

  • Linnet, J., et al. (2011). "Dopamine release in ventral striatum during Iowa Gambling Task performance is associated with increased excitement levels in pathological gambling." Addiction, 106(2), 383-390.
  • Problem gamblers show elevated dopamine response during gambling, particularly during near-miss events.

Gaming Regulation:

  • State and territory gaming authorities regulate poker machines in Australia, including: Victorian Commission for Gambling and Liquor Regulation, Liquor & Gaming NSW, Office of Liquor and Gaming Regulation (QLD), and equivalent bodies in other jurisdictions.
  • Gaming machine standards are set by organisations including Gaming Technologies Association Australia.

Chapter 3: Social Manipulation

Social Networks and Gambling:

  • Dowling, N. A., et al. (2016). "The impact of social networks and social support on problem gambling." Journal of Gambling Studies, 32(2), 485-497.
  • Australian Institute of Family Studies (2017). "Gambling and families." AIFS Research Report.
  • Social bonds centred on gambling venues can reinforce problematic gambling behaviour and reduce treatment-seeking.

Chapter 4-6: Loyalty Systems and Costs

VIP Programs and Escalation:

  • Hing, N., et al. (2014). "A review of research on interventions for problem gambling." Victorian Responsible Gambling Foundation.
  • Loyalty programs and tiered reward systems increase spending through sunk cost fallacy exploitation and loss-chasing behaviour.

Chapter 7-8: Australian Gambling Statistics

National Gambling Data:

  • Queensland Government Statistician's Office (2021). "Australian Gambling Statistics 2020-21."
  • Australians lost approximately $24.8 billion to gambling in 2020-21 financial year.
  • Poker machines account for approximately $15 billion annually (roughly 60% of total gambling losses).
  • Australia has approximately 195,000 poker machines (one of the highest per-capita rates globally).

Socioeconomic Disparities:

  • Alliance for Gambling Reform (2019-2023). Various reports on pokies losses by postcode.
  • The most disadvantaged postcodes experience disproportionately high per-adult losses to poker machines compared to wealthy areas.
  • Poker machine density is significantly higher in disadvantaged communities.

Prevalence of Gambling Harm:

  • Australian Institute of Family Studies (2020). "Gambling harm in Australia."
  • Productivity Commission (2010). "Gambling Inquiry Report."
  • Estimates suggest 0.5-5% of Australian adults experience gambling harm, depending on measurement methodology.
  • Approximately 5-10 additional people are negatively affected for each person directly experiencing gambling harm.

Chapter 9-10: Treatment and Recovery

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy:

  • Cowlishaw, S., et al. (2012). "Psychological therapies for pathological and problem gambling." Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews.
  • Petry, N. M., et al. (2017). "Cognitive-behavioral therapy for pathological gamblers." Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology.
  • CBT approaches targeting understanding of randomness, odds, and cognitive biases show effectiveness in reducing gambling behaviour.

Mindfulness-Based Techniques:

  • Marlatt, G. A., & Kristeller, J. L. (1999). "Mindfulness and meditation." In W. R. Miller (Ed.), Integrating spirituality into treatment. American Psychological Association.
  • Urge-surfing and mindfulness-based techniques are effective components of addiction treatment.

Self-Exclusion Programs:

  • All Australian states and territories operate venue-based self-exclusion programs.
  • BetStop (www.betstop.gov.au) is the national self-exclusion register for online and phone wagering, launched by the Australian Government.

Chapter 11-12: Mental Health Comorbidity

Associated Mental Health Issues:

  • Lorains, F. K., et al. (2011). "Prevalence of comorbid psychiatric disorders in problem and pathological gambling." American Journal of Psychiatry, 168(8), 795-803.
  • Problem gambling shows high comorbidity with depression, anxiety, substance use disorders, and elevated suicide risk.

Author's Note on Sources

This book combines peer-reviewed research, government statistics, regulatory findings, and the author's lived experience. Where specific research findings are cited, they represent well-documented phenomena in gambling studies literature. Statistical claims reference official Australian gambling data sources. Personal experiences and strategic analysis represent the author's perspectives based on both research and lived experience with poker machine addiction.

Readers seeking specific citations for particular claims should consult the research databases of major gambling studies journals including: Journal of Gambling Studies, Addiction, International Gambling Studies, and reports from Australian gambling reform organisations and government statistical offices.

Important: Treatment recommendations in this book are informed by evidence-based practices but are not a substitute for professional help. Readers experiencing gambling harm should consult qualified professionals.
