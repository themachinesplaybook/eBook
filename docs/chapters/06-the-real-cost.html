Concealing the True Price: How Machines Keep Players Focused on Wins

Dave

Not in individual sessions, but across the industry.

How do we design systems that keep players from seeing the real cost?

Eve

That's a critical element, Dave.

If players truly understood the mathematics working against them, many would walk away.

So we need to obscure the real cost while emphasizing the wins. Let's start with the basics‚Äîhow do we display information on the screen?

Dave

First, we never show total amount spent.

Ever.

We show credits, not dollars.

If someone's playing $2 spins, we don't show '$2 per spin'‚Äîwe show '1 credit per spin.' The player has to do mental math to convert credits back to actual money, and most people won't bother.

Eve

Exactly.

And we make the credit values confusing. Maybe one credit equals $2.50 or $1.75‚Äîsomething that's not easy to calculate in your head.

The cognitive load of converting credits to real money creates a psychological distance between the player and their actual spending.

Dave

We should celebrate wins in credits too, but big, bold numbers. 'You won 40 credits!' sounds impressive.

The fact that 40 credits only equals $100, and they've already spent $300 to get it, is information we don't highlight.

Eve

And when they do win, we display it as a gross amount, never net.

If they bet $5 and win $3, the machine celebrates 'YOU WON $3!' with lights and sounds. It doesn't say 'You lost $2.'

Dave

Right.

The machine never tracks losses in any visible way. There's no running total showing 'Amount inserted: $450, Amount won: $85, Net loss: $365.' That information would be devastating to continued play.

So we hide it completely.

Eve

What about the venue level? How do we conceal the bigger picture‚Äîthe scale of what flows through these machines daily?

Dave

No information boards showing house take percentages.

No displays showing how much money was inserted versus paid out today.

And definitely no comparisons showing what players could have bought with the money they've lost.

Eve

We should talk about the industry numbers too, because they're staggering and most players never see them.

Australia has more than 200,000 Pokie machines across the country‚Äîthe highest concentration per capita in the world. Australians lost over $32 billion to gambling in 2024, with approximately $12-14.5 billion of that going directly to pokies.

Dave

And the daily turnover is mind-blowing.

An average machine in a high-traffic venue can take in thousands of dollars per day. Multiply that by tens of thousands of machines, and you're looking at millions flowing through these machines daily.

Eve

The payout rate is particularly deceptive.

Machines might have a 90% return to player rate.

That sounds reasonable, right? But what it means is that over the lifetime of the machine, 10% of everything inserted stays with the house. On billions of dollars, that 10% represents enormous profit extracted from communities.

Dave

And that 90% doesn't mean each player gets back 90% of what they spend.

It means that across all plays over time, 90% gets recycled back into the pool of players.

Some people win big, but their wins are funded by everyone else's losses.

The house always keeps its 10%, guaranteed.

Eve

We should never explain this to players.

If they understood that every $100 they insert has an expected return of $90, and that's before accounting for the time value of money, taxes on wins, or the opportunity cost of what else they could have done with that money‚Äîthey'd see it's mathematically impossible to profit long-term.

Dave

So how do we keep them from doing that math? Time pressure, for one.

The machines need to operate quickly‚Äîspins every few seconds. No time to sit back and calculate lifetime losses. enough time to press the button again.

Eve

And we use intermittent wins to disrupt any mental accounting they're trying to do. when they're starting to think 'I've lost a lot today,' we give them a $50 win.

Their mental ledger resets to that win, not their net position.

Dave

What about the opportunity cost? That's something that got to me later‚Äîrealising what I could have done with all that money instead of feeding it into machines.

Eve

We definitely don't want players thinking about opportunity cost.

No messages like 'You've spent $500 today‚Äîthat could have been a weekend away with your family.' We keep their focus entirely on the game, the next spin, the potential win. Never on alternative uses for their money.

Dave

And we design the environment to minimize price sensitivity.

Free drinks, comfortable seating, ambient music‚Äîwe make the venue feel like a place where normal rules about money don't apply. Like a casino, where spending $500 on entertainment is part of the experience, not a significant financial decision with real consequences.

Eve

The ATMs are crucial too.

They need to be in the venue, easy to access, no prominent warnings about gambling harm. Quick withdrawal, get back to playing.

The less friction between 'I'm out of money' and 'I have more money to play with,' the better for continued play.

Dave

And those ATMs should definitely not display running totals of how much someone has withdrawn that session.

Can you imagine if the ATM said 'This is your fourth withdrawal today, totaling $400. Are you sure?' That would break the spell instantly.

Eve

We need to keep the focus on wins, possibilities, and potential.

The actual mathematics, the real costs, the opportunity costs, the industry scale‚Äîall of that stays hidden. Players should feel like they're having fun, possibly winning, part of an entertaining experience. Not like they're systematically transferring their wealth to a corporation that's designed every element of this experience to extract maximum money from them.

Dave

It's grim when you lay it all out.

We're designing a system that deliberately obscures financial reality from people who are making significant financial decisions.

If any other industry did this‚Äîhid the true cost, used confusing pricing, celebrated small returns on large investments‚Äîwe'd call it predatory.

Eve

And yet in gambling, it's standard operating procedure.

The concealment of true cost isn't a bug‚Äîit's a feature. It's essential to keeping players engaged long past the point where rational analysis would tell them to stop.

Counting the Real Cost: Understanding What You're Losing

When I finally sat down to calculate my

total losses‚Äîthat over $200,000 I mentioned earlier‚ÄîI made a mistake that probably kept me in denial for longer than it should have.

I only counted the money. Don't get me wrong, over $200,000 is a devastating financial loss. That's life-changing money.

But it wasn't the real cost. It wasn't even close to the real cost.

The real cost was what I could have done with that money, the time I lost, the relationships I damaged, the opportunities I missed, the person I became in service to those machines.

Let me break down what real cost means, because until you count all of it, you won't fully understand what you're losing.

The Visible Financial Cost

Let's start with the money, because that's the most obvious and easiest to track (even though the machines work hard to prevent you from tracking it). Here's what you need to do: calculate your total gambling losses. Not estimates, not rough guesses. Real numbers from bank statements, credit card records, ATM withdrawals, cash advances, loans.

For many people, this will be the first time they've ever looked at the total.

The machines and venues don't make this easy.

They give you account statements showing points earned and rewards claimed, but not net financial position.

You need to create a spreadsheet with three columns:

  1. Money inserted (all forms‚Äîcash, card, withdrawals at the venue)
  2. Money won (actual cash you walked away with, not credits played back into machines)
  3. Net position (insert minus won) Be honest. Include everything. That $50 you borrowed from a mate.

That credit card advance you took out.

That rent money you 'borrowed' from your account.

All of it.

The number will likely shock you. It shocked me.

But seeing it is essential because you can't address a problem you won't quantify.

The Opportunity Cost

Now here's where it gets painful.

For every dollar you've lost gambling, there's an alternative reality where you spent that money on something else. That's opportunity cost‚Äîwhat you gave up to gamble. Take your total losses and do these calculations: Investment value: If you'd put that money in a conservative managed fund averaging 7% annual return, what would it be worth now? For my over $200,000 over roughly 10 years, that's approximately $397,000.

I didn't lose $200K‚ÄîI lost nearly $400K in future value. Debt elimination: How much of your mortgage could you have paid off? At average mortgage rates, paying an extra $20,000 per year toward principal saves you far more in interest over the life of the loan. My gambling money could have cut years off my mortgage and saved tens of thousands in interest. Emergency fund: Financial advisors recommend 6 months of expenses in emergency savings. Could your gambling losses have fully funded that safety net? Mine could have built an emergency fund four times over. Education: Could you have funded your kids' university education? Paid for professional development that would have advanced your career? My losses could have paid for two full university degrees with money left over. Retirement: What would your gambling losses look like if invested in superannuation over time? With compound growth and tax benefits, that money could have been hundreds of thousands in retirement savings. Write these down. do the math. See the houses you could have bought, the debt you could have eliminated, the security you could have built. See all the things you traded for the fleeting excitement of spinning reels.

The Time Cost

Now let's talk about something harder to quantify but equally devastating‚Äîtime. Calculate how many hours you've spent gambling. Be honest. Include travel time to and from venues, time spent at machines, time spent thinking about gambling when you weren't physically there.

For me, I averaged probably 15 hours per week in my peak years. That's 780 hours per year. Over 10 years, that's 7,800 hours‚Äîthe equivalent of nearly four full-time work years.

Now think about what else you could have done with 7,800 hours:

  • Learned multiple languages fluently
  • Become genuinely skilled at a musical instrument
  • Built a business
  • Written several books
  • Earned a degree or professional qualification
  • Been present for your kids' childhoods
  • Deepened your relationships
  • Pursued hobbies and passions
  • Volunteered in your community
  • rested and recovered from life's demands Those hours are gone.

You can't get them back.

Every hour at a Pokie machine is an hour not spent building the life you want.

The Relationship Cost

This is the hardest cost to quantify, but for many people it's the most devastating.

What has gambling cost you in relationships? Count the lies you've told.

The money you've hidden.

The events you've missed or been distracted during.

The broken promises.

The trust that's been damaged or destroyed. Count the arguments about money.

The stress you've put on your partner who watches you destroy your financial security.

The example you've set for your kids about what matters and how to handle money. Count the friendships that have faded because you were spending all your time at the venue.

The family gatherings you skipped or left early to gamble.

The intimate moments with your partner you traded for time at a machine.

I missed my daughter's Year 10 school play because I was chasing losses at the venue.

I told my wife I had to work late when I was at the Pokie lounge.

I borrowed money from my brother and lied about why I needed it.

I chose machines over people, repeatedly, for years.

You can't put a dollar value on a relationship, but you can count the damage. Make a list. Write down every relationship that's been harmed by your gambling. See the full scope of what you've traded for the illusion of winning.

The Health Cost

The stress of financial losses takes a physical toll.

The hours spent sedentary at machines affects your health.

The sleep you lose worrying about money.

The meals you skip while gambling.

The exercise you don't do because you're exhausted from late nights at the venue. Research published in the Journal of Gambling Studies found that problem gamblers have significantly higher rates of stress-related illnesses, depression, anxiety, and substance use disorders. The International Journal of Mental Health and Addiction found elevated suicide risk among problem gamblers compared to the general population. Consider your health since you started gambling seriously. Are you more stressed? Less fit? Sleeping worse? Using alcohol or other substances more? These are costs too, and they're devastating.

The Career Cost

Have you missed work due to gambling? Shown up exhausted and performed poorly? Passed on advancement opportunities because your finances or mental state couldn't support them? Failed to invest in professional development because gambling consumed your discretionary income? I turned down a promotion that would have required relocation because I was too financially unstable from gambling to afford the move.

That decision probably cost me hundreds of thousands in lifetime earnings.

That's a real cost of gambling, even though it doesn't show up in any loss statement.

The Identity Cost

Finally, there's the cost of who you've become.

The person who lies.

The person who breaks promises.

The person who chooses machines over family.

The person who can't be trusted with money.

The person who's always chasing, always stressed, always one bad session away from crisis. That's not who you wanted to be. That's not who you are at your core.

But gambling has created this version of you, and maintaining that false self has a cost in dignity, self-respect, and integrity.

Defensive Strategies for Understanding True Cost

Strategy 1: Create a Total Cost Ledger

Make a comprehensive document that captures all these costs:

  • Total financial losses
  • Opportunity cost calculations
  • Hours spent
  • Relationships harmed
  • Health impacts
  • Career setbacks
  • Identity changes Update it monthly. See the costs accumulate.

This visibility makes it much harder to minimize or deny the real impact.

Strategy 2: Visualise the Alternative

For every dollar you're about to spend gambling, picture what else you could do with it. That $50 could be groceries, a nice meal with your partner, a contribution to your kid's education fund, a payment toward debt. Keep a photo on your phone of something you're saving for‚Äîa family holiday, a home renovation, financial security. Before you insert money into a machine, look at that photo. See the choice clearly: this, or that.

Strategy 3: Track Daily Opportunity Costs

When you resist the urge to gamble, immediately transfer the money you would have spent into a separate savings account. Watch that account grow. See the visible, tangible result of not gambling.

This creates a competing reward system. Instead of the intermittent, unpredictable wins from machines, you get consistent, guaranteed wins by not playing.

Every day you don't gamble, you win the amount you would have spent.

Strategy 4: Share Your Numbers

Tell someone you trust your total losses. Say it out loud. Show them the calculations.

The shame might be intense, but shame thrives in secrecy. Exposure diminishes its power.

When someone else knows your numbers, you become accountable.

You can't minimize or deny it to yourself as easily when someone else is watching you make different choices.

The Industry's True Cost to Australia

Let's zoom out from your personal costs to the societal level, because understanding the scale of this industry helps you see that your experience isn't isolated‚Äîit's part of a massive wealth extraction system. Australians lost approximately $32 billion to gambling in 2024, with pokies accounting for $12-14.5 billion of that total. That's over $12 billion removed from Australian communities, households, and individuals, transferred to gambling corporations and their shareholders‚Äîand this figure continues to grow year after year. The Australian Institute of Family Studies estimates that approximately 5% of Australian adults experience gambling harm. With an adult population of about 20 million, that's one million Australians suffering harm from gambling.

But the harm isn't evenly distributed. Research shows gambling venues are disproportionately located in lower socioeconomic areas.

The communities that can least afford losses are targeted most heavily. A report by the Alliance for Gambling Reform found that the 10 most disadvantaged postcodes in Australia lost an average of $31,000 per adult to Pokies in a single year. The 10 wealthiest postcodes lost an average of $4,000 per adult.

This isn't entertainment. It's systematic wealth extraction from vulnerable communities.

The Scale of Australia's Pokie Crisis: By the Numbers (2024)

To truly understand the magnitude of this crisis, here are the current figures that reveal just how deeply pokies have embedded themselves into Australian society:

National Gambling Losses:

  • Total gambling losses across Australia in 2024: $32 billion

  • Australia has 20% of the world's poker machines despite having just 0.3% of the global population
  • We have more pokie machines per capita than any other country in the world

Turnover (Money Flowing Through Machines):

  • Total turnover through pokies in 2024: $191.1 billion
  • This is the total amount inserted into machines before wins are paid back
  • The scale is staggering‚Äîover $500 million flows through these machines every single day

State-by-State Breakdown (2024):

  • New South Wales: $8.64 billion lost to pokies (highest in the nation)
  • Victoria: Over $3 billion lost to pokies
  • Queensland: $2.8 billion lost to pokies
  • South Australia: Approximately $1 billion lost to pokies
  • Northern Territory: $185 million lost to pokies

  • State and territory governments collected over $6 billion in gambling taxes in 2024
  • This creates a perverse incentive‚Äîgovernments now depend on gambling losses to fund services
  • For every dollar "invested" in gambling harm minimization, governments collect hundreds in gambling tax revenue

Social and Economic Costs:

  • The Victorian Responsible Gambling Foundation estimates the social cost of gambling harm in Victoria alone at $14 billion annually
  • This includes relationship breakdown, mental health costs, crime, bankruptcy, lost productivity

Machine Performance:

Problem Gambling Prevalence:

These aren't abstract numbers. Each billion dollars represents tens of thousands of personal stories like mine‚Äîfamilies destroyed, careers derailed, lives consumed by machines engineered to extract maximum wealth.

When you see these figures, remember: this is by design. The industry is working exactly as intended.

The True Cost of 'Entertainment'

The gambling industry frames losses as 'the cost of entertainment.' Spending $200 on a night out is paying for the experience, right?

Here's the difference: when you pay $200 for concert tickets, you get the concert.

When you spend $200 on a nice dinner, you get the dinner.

When you lose $200 to Pokies, you get nothing except the temporary neurological stimulation from variable ratio reinforcement and near-misses.

And unlike genuine entertainment, gambling 'entertainment' is designed to extract as much money as possible through psychological manipulation.

You didn't freely choose to spend $200 on a fun evening‚Äîyou were exploited for $200 by a system engineered to override your rational decision-making.

Counting the Cost Going Forward

The costs we've discussed‚Äîfinancial, opportunity, time, relationship, health, career, identity‚Äîare real. They're not abstract. They're not exaggerated.

If anything, most people underestimate their true costs because facing them fully is too painful.

But here's the thing: every day you continue gambling, these costs increase.

The hole gets deeper.

The opportunity costs compound.

The relationships suffer more.

The time disappears faster.

The only way to stop the bleeding is to stop playing. Not play less, not play more carefully, not chase your losses back‚Äîstop completely.

When you truly understand the real cost of gambling‚Äîwhen you see all the money, time, relationships, health, opportunities, and identity lost‚Äîthe machines lose their appeal.

The flashing lights and celebratory sounds become markers of destruction, not entertainment. You're not losing the money you insert. You're losing the life you could have had. Count the real cost. See it clearly. Then decide if the next spin is worth adding to that total. Because that's the only question that matters: Is this worth what it's costing me? For me, the answer‚Äîonce I finally saw the real cost‚Äîwas devastatingly, obviously, no.
