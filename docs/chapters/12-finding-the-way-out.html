Discovering Your Own Way Out: Tools to Reclaim Your Life

Everything Eve and I just discussed represents the ideal world; where gambling venues actually prioritised harm reduction over profit. Where machines were designed to help people stop rather than keep playing. Where intervention was proactive instead of invisible.

That world doesn't exist.

So you need to build your own exit strategy without help from the venues that profit from your continued play. You need to create the intervention systems, support structures, and accountability mechanisms that should exist but don't.

This is the most important chapter in this book. Everything else has been exposing the traps. Now we're building the escape route.

The Six-Phase Exit Plan

Phase 1: Recognition and Documentation (Week 1)

Before you can escape, you need to see the trap clearly. Spend one week documenting everything:

Daily gambling log:

  • Time spent gambling
  • Money inserted vs. money won
  • Emotional state before, during, and after
  • Triggers that led you to gamble
  • Thoughts about stopping vs. continuing

Total cost calculation:

  • Review bank statements, credit cards, loans for the past year
  • Calculate total net loss (not gross spending; actual money lost)
  • Calculate opportunity costs using the methods from Chapter 5
  • List relationship, health, and career costs

Trigger identification:

  • What situations lead you to gamble? (stress, boredom, loneliness, celebration)
  • What times are highest risk? (after work, weekends, payday)
  • What emotions precede gambling? (anxiety, excitement, frustration)

Don't try to change your behaviour yet. Just observe and document. You can't create an effective exit plan without understanding your specific patterns.

Phase 2: Creating Your Support Network (Week 2)

Escaping alone is exponentially harder than escaping with support. Build your network:

Professional support:

  • Contact Gambling Help Online: 1800 858 858 (available 24/7)
  • Search for a counselor specialising in gambling addiction
  • Consider joining Gamblers Anonymous or a similar support group
  • Book an appointment with a financial counselor to address debt

Personal support:

  • Identify 2-3 trusted people to tell about your decision to quit
  • Ask one person to be your primary accountability partner
  • Give your accountability partner permission to ask hard questions and check bank statements
  • Schedule regular check-ins (daily for first month, then weekly)

Practical support:

  • Install gambling-blocking apps: Betblocker, Gamban, or similar
  • Add gambling venues to blocked locations on your GPS
  • Give control of finances to your partner or trusted person temporarily if possible
  • Remove access to credit cards and keep minimal cash

The mistake most people make is trying to quit through willpower alone. Willpower depletes. Support systems don't.

Phase 3: Environmental Redesign (Week 3)

You need to eliminate or reduce exposure to gambling triggers:

Physical environment:

  • Delete all gambling apps from your phone
  • Unsubscribe from all venue promotional emails and texts
  • Block gambling websites using browser extensions
  • Change your route home/to work if it passes gambling venues
  • Find new locations for social activities that aren't near venues

Time management:

  • Identify your highest-risk gambling times
  • Schedule competing activities during those times
  • Book commitments that create accountability (classes, appointments, social plans)
  • Fill the time you used to spend gambling with concrete alternatives

Financial barriers:

  • Cancel all VIP memberships and loyalty programs
  • Register for venue self-exclusion programs in your state
  • Set up automatic transfers from your paycheck to savings
  • Remove ATM/credit cards from your wallet before leaving home
  • Carry only the minimum cash needed for each day

Phase 4: The 30-Day Abstinence Challenge (Weeks 4-7)

Commit to complete abstinence for 30 days. Not reduced gambling; zero gambling. Here's why: you need to break the behavioural patterns completely and prove to yourself that life without gambling is possible.

Daily practices:

  • Morning: Read your cost calculation from Phase 1
  • Throughout day: Use the urge-surfing technique when cravings hit (described below)
  • Evening: Update your gambling-free day counter and celebrate the win
  • Weekly: Meet with your accountability partner and review your week

Urge-surfing technique:

When you feel the urge to gamble, use this four-step process:

  1. Pause: Stop what you're doing. Don't act on the urge immediately.
  2. Observe: Notice the physical sensations of the craving. Where do you feel it in your body? What thoughts are accompanying it?
  3. Breathe: Take 10 slow, deep breaths. Count them. Focus entirely on breathing.
  4. Decide: After breathing, consciously decide. Don't act automatically.

Most urges peak and then decline within 15-20 minutes if you don't act on them. Urge-surfing lets the craving pass without surrendering to it.

Alternative reward system:

For every day you don't gamble, transfer the amount you would have spent to a "freedom fund" savings account. Watch it grow. At 30 days, use some of it for a meaningful reward; something that actually adds value to your life.

Phase 5: Relationship Repair and Life Building (Ongoing)

Gambling likely damaged relationships and prevented you from building the life you want. Now you repair and build:

Relationship repair:

  • Have honest conversations with people you've hurt or lied to
  • Acknowledge specific harms without making excuses
  • Ask what you can do to rebuild trust
  • Accept that some relationships may not be repairable
  • Focus on consistent trustworthy behaviour moving forward

Life building:

  • Identify three areas of life you want to develop (career, health, hobbies, relationships)
  • Set specific, measurable goals in each area
  • Allocate the time and money you're saving from not gambling toward these goals
  • Track progress and celebrate milestones

Finding your third place:

Remember from Chapter 3: you need a healthy third place to replace the Pokie lounge. This is crucial. Options:

  • Recreational community (pool hall, darts, lawn bowls, sports team)
  • Creative pursuits (art class, writing group, music)
  • Volunteer work (animal shelter, community garden, charity)
  • Hobby groups (photography, cooking, gaming, book clubs)
  • Spiritual or philosophical communities (church, meditation groups)

The key is finding belonging and connection around something healthy and constructive.

Phase 6: Relapse Prevention and Long-Term Maintenance (Ongoing)

Most people who quit gambling will experience at least one relapse. The goal is recognising warning signs early and having a plan:

Warning signs of impending relapse:

  • Romanticising past gambling ("Remember that time I won $800?")
  • Minimising harm ("It wasn't really that bad")
  • Isolating from support network
  • Stopping attendance at support groups
  • Keeping secrets from accountability partners
  • Increased stress without healthy coping mechanisms
  • Visiting gambling venues "just to see friends"
  • Testing yourself ("I'll just watch, not play")

Relapse response plan:

If you gamble again, follow this protocol immediately:

  1. Stop: Leave the venue immediately. Don't try to win back losses.
  2. Contact support: Call your accountability partner or Gambling Help Online right away.
  3. Document: Write down what triggered the relapse, how you felt, what you lost.
  4. Forgive: A relapse is a setback, not a failure. Recovery isn't linear.
  5. Analyze: What failed in your system? What additional support or changes do you need?
  6. Restart: Begin a new abstinence period immediately. Don't wait for Monday or next month.
  7. Strengthen: Add additional layers of protection based on what you learned.

Self-Exclusion Programs:

Each Australian state/territory has self-exclusion programs where you can ban yourself from venues:

  • New South Wales: Self-Exclusion Program via Liquor & Gaming NSW
  • Victoria: YourPlay and BetStop (national self-exclusion register)
  • Queensland: Self Exclusion Program via Office of Liquor and Gaming Regulation
  • South Australia: Statewide Self-Exclusion scheme
  • Western Australia: Self-Exclusion programs through Department of Local Government, Sport and Cultural Industries
  • Tasmania: Self-Exclusion through Tasmanian Liquor and Gaming Commission
  • ACT: Problem Gambling Assistance Fund
  • Northern Territory: Self-Exclusion through Licensing NT

BetStop (www.betstop.gov.au) is the national self-exclusion register that covers online and phone wagering across Australia.

Blocking Technology:

  • Gamban: Blocks all gambling sites/apps across all your devices
  • BetBlocker: Free open-source gambling blocking software
  • Cold Turkey: General website and app blocker
  • Freedom: Cross-platform app and website blocking
  • Covenant Eyes: Internet accountability software

Financial Management:

  • ASIC's MoneySmart: Free financial guidance and budgeting tools (moneysmart.gov.au)
  • National Debt Helpline: 1800 007 007
  • Financial Counselling: Free service available through community organizations
  • Budget Planner Apps: Pocketbook, MoneyBrilliant, or YNAB

Mental Health Support:

Many people gambling problematically are also dealing with anxiety, depression, trauma, or other mental health challenges. Addressing these underlying issues is crucial for long-term recovery:

  • Psychologist or Counselor: Ask your GP for a Mental Health Care Plan (allows Medicare rebates for up to 10 sessions)
  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy: Particularly effective for gambling addiction
  • Mindfulness and Meditation: Apps like Headspace, Calm, or Smiling Mind

What Success Looks Like

Success isn't perfection. It's not never thinking about gambling again. It's not having zero struggle.

Success is:

  • Reducing the frequency and severity of gambling
  • Developing healthier coping mechanisms for stress and emotion
  • Rebuilding financial stability and damaged relationships
  • Finding meaning and connection outside of gambling
  • Having more good days than bad days
  • Maintaining hope even through setbacks

For some people, success means complete abstinence forever. For others, it means occasional controlled play that doesn't interfere with life functioning. You need to be honest with yourself about which category you're in; most problem gamblers can't sustain controlled play and are better off with complete abstinence.

My Personal Experience

I tried to quit gambling at least a dozen times before it finally stuck. I'd go weeks or even months without playing, then relapse. Each time I learned something about my triggers, my weaknesses, my needs.

What finally worked for me:

  • Complete honesty with my partner about the full extent of losses
  • Weekly meetings with a counselor specialising in addiction
  • Gamblers Anonymous meetings (even though I felt awkward initially)
  • Transferring financial control to my partner for six months
  • Self-exclusion from all venues in my area
  • Finding a new third place (a pool hall where I played eight ball) that gave me the community I'd been getting from the Pokie lounge
  • Addressing the underlying anxiety and depression that gambling was medicating

It took two years before I felt truly free from the compulsion. Five years before I could walk past a venue without feeling the pull. Ten years later, I still sometimes think about it, but the thoughts are manageable and don't control my behaviour.

Recovery is possible. It's hard, but it's possible. And it's worth every bit of effort.

The app could facilitate legacy planning too. "Write a letter to yourself explaining why you want to quit. Record a video of yourself at your lowest point. When you're tempted to relapse, the app will show you these reminders of what you're fighting for."

Dave

So in moments of weakness, you're confronted with your own voice from a moment of clarity, reminding you why this matters.

Eve

We could add family features. Family members could have limited access to see if their loved one is struggling and needs support, with permission from the primary user. Not surveillance, but consensual accountability.

Dave

That would address one of the biggest challenges in addiction; the isolation and secrecy. If families can see warning signs and offer support early, before crisis develops, recovery outcomes would improve.

Eve

This app would essentially be using all the sophisticated technology and behavioural science that gambling companies use to trap people, but inverting the purpose entirely. Instead of optimising for maximum engagement and spending, optimising for maximum protection and recovery.

Dave

The technology exists. The behavioural science exists. The only thing missing is the incentive to build it. Gambling companies won't build tools that help people quit because it would reduce their revenue. So it would need to come from public health organizations, governments, or independent developers.

Eve

But if such tools existed and were widely adopted, they could dramatically reduce gambling harm. Not through willpower or moral exhortation, but through smart technology that protects people from manipulation and supports them through recovery.

Leveraging Technology for Self-Control: Techniques to Break the Addiction

Everything Eve and I just described represents the ideal app: a comprehensive AI-powered recovery support tool that doesn't yet exist in complete form. But pieces of it do exist, and you can start using technology today to support your recovery.

Here's what's actually available now and how to use it effectively.

Currently Available Tools

Gambling Blocking Software:

Gamban (www.gamban.com)

  • Blocks all gambling sites and apps across all devices
  • Can't be easily bypassed or uninstalled
  • Costs about $25/year
  • Available for computers, phones, tablets
  • Covers thousands of gambling sites internationally

BetBlocker (betblocker.org)

  • Free, open-source gambling blocking
  • Blocks websites and apps
  • Less comprehensive than Gamban but free
  • Good option if cost is a barrier

Cold Turkey (getcoldturkey.com)

  • General purpose website and app blocker
  • You create custom blocklists including gambling sites
  • Can set schedules (block gambling sites during high-risk hours)
  • Can lock blocks so they can't be bypassed during weak moments

How to use them effectively:

  • Install on ALL devices, not just your primary phone
  • Set them up during a moment of clarity, not during urges
  • Tell your accountability partner when you've installed them
  • Don't rely on them alone; they're one layer of protection, not a complete solution
  • Use multiple blockers together for redundancy

Financial Control Apps

PocketGuard (pocketguard.com)

  • Tracks all spending automatically
  • Shows how much you can safely spend
  • Identifies spending patterns including gambling
  • Helps you stick to budgets

YNAB (You Need A Budget) (youneedabudget.com)

  • Comprehensive budgeting system
  • Makes you assign every dollar a job
  • Harder to justify gambling when you've explicitly allocated money elsewhere
  • Has educational content about financial decision-making

MoneyBrilliant (moneybrilliant.com.au)

  • Australian-specific financial tracking
  • Categorises spending automatically
  • Shows gambling spending clearly
  • Free basic version available

How to use them effectively:

  • Connect all your accounts for complete visibility
  • Check the app before making any significant financial decision
  • Set up alerts for unusual spending patterns
  • Share access with your accountability partner if you're comfortable
  • Use the data to calculate your true cost of gambling

Accountability and Support Apps

I Am Sober (iamsober.com)

  • Track days since last gambled
  • Daily pledges and motivational messages
  • Community support features
  • Progress milestones and achievements
  • Can track multiple addictions/habits simultaneously

Nomo (nomoapp.org)

  • Sobriety clock showing time abstinent
  • Chip system based on recovery milestones
  • Private or social options
  • Daily accountability check-ins

AccountaBuddy

  • Pairs you with accountability partners
  • Scheduled check-ins via text
  • Track habits and goals together
  • Social accountability features

How to use them effectively:

  • Update them daily, even when (especially when) you don't want to
  • Share your milestones with support network
  • Use the community features to connect with others in recovery
  • Set up daily reminders to check in
  • Celebrate milestones meaningfully

Meditation and Mental Health Apps

Headspace (headspace.com)

  • Guided meditations for various purposes
  • Specific content for managing cravings and urges
  • Sleepcasts for better sleep
  • Mindfulness exercises for daily life

Calm (calm.com)

  • Meditation and relaxation
  • Sleep stories and music
  • Breathing exercises for stress management
  • Mental health resources

Smiling Mind (smilingmind.com.au)

  • Australian mindfulness app
  • Completely free
  • Programs for stress, sleep, relationships
  • Age-appropriate content for whole family

How to use them effectively:

  • Use them preventatively, not just during crisis
  • Start with just 5 minutes daily
  • Build a consistent practice during early recovery
  • Use the breathing exercises when urges hit
  • Make mindfulness part of your daily routine

Location and Geofencing Tools

Life360 (life360.com)

  • Family location sharing
  • Set up geofences around gambling venues
  • Alerts when you enter high-risk areas
  • Your support person can see if you're near venues

Google Maps Custom Lists

  • Mark all gambling venues in your area
  • Create custom routes that avoid them
  • Set up alerts when approaching marked areas

How to use them effectively:

  • Be honest about all the venues you visit
  • Give a support person access to your location during high-risk times
  • Use them to plan routes that avoid temptation
  • Don't disable them during moments of weakness; that's when you need them most

DIY Tech Solutions

Even without specialised apps, you can use common technology for recovery support:

Calendar Blocking:

  • Schedule activities during high-risk times
  • Set recurring appointments that conflict with typical gambling times
  • Use calendar alerts to remind you of your recovery commitment

Note-Taking Apps:

  • Keep a daily recovery journal in Evernote, OneNote, or similar
  • Write down triggers, urges, successes, struggles
  • Review weekly to identify patterns

Email Filters:

  • Set up filters to automatically delete gambling venue promotions
  • Block sender domains from venues and gambling sites
  • Unsubscribe from all gambling-related communications

Photo Reminders:

  • Take photos of your "why"; family, goals, dreams
  • Set them as phone wallpaper
  • Put them in places you'll see during temptation moments

Voice Memos:

  • Record yourself during moments of clarity explaining why you want to quit
  • Play them back during moments of temptation
  • Update them as your recovery progresses

Building Your Technology Stack

Don't try to implement everything at once. Build gradually:

Week 1:

  • Install gambling blocking software on all devices
  • Set up a spending tracker
  • Download one sobriety tracking app

Week 2:

  • Set up geofencing around local venues
  • Install a meditation app and begin daily practice
  • Create email filters for gambling promotions

Week 3:

  • Share location access with accountability partner
  • Set up calendar blocking for high-risk times
  • Begin daily journaling in a notes app

Week 4:

  • Review what's working and what isn't
  • Adjust or add tools based on your specific needs
  • Make technology use routine and automatic

The Limitations of Technology

Technology is powerful, but it's not magic. It can't:

  • Replace human connection and support
  • Make decisions for you
  • Force you to change if you're not ready
  • Address underlying mental health issues alone
  • Guarantee success

What technology CAN do:

  • Make harmful behaviours harder to engage in
  • Provide visibility into patterns you might not otherwise see
  • Connect you to support and resources quickly
  • Create accountability mechanisms
  • Remind you of your goals during weak moments
  • Track progress and celebrate successes

Think of technology as a force multiplier for your recovery efforts. It makes your willpower more effective, your support network more responsive, your awareness more comprehensive.

But the core work; deciding to change, doing the difficult emotional processing, building new habits, repairing relationships; that's human work that technology can only support, not replace.

My Personal Technology Strategy

When I was getting serious about quitting, I built a comprehensive tech stack:

  1. Gamban on all devices to block gambling sites
  2. Life360 sharing my location with my partner
  3. YNAB for complete financial visibility
  4. I Am Sober for daily check-ins and tracking
  5. Headspace for daily meditation practice
  6. Google Calendar with recurring blocks during my high-risk evening hours
  7. OneNote for daily recovery journaling
  8. Custom iPhone shortcuts that, when triggered, would text my partner "I'm struggling and near a venue, please call me"

Did this technology stack prevent all urges? No. Did it prevent me from gambling? Not by itself. But it created layers of protection that made acting on urges much harder. It gave me time; precious seconds and minutes between urge and action; where I could make a different choice.

That time was everything. Without the technology creating friction and forcing pause, I would have acted impulsively hundreds of times. The technology gave me space to choose differently.

The Future of Recovery Technology

The comprehensive AI-powered recovery app Eve and I designed earlier doesn't fully exist yet, but pieces of it are being developed. The trajectory is toward more sophisticated, personalised, proactive recovery support tools.

As these tools develop, adopt them. The gambling industry invests millions in optimising their manipulation systems. You should use every available tool to counter that manipulation.

But don't wait for perfect technology. Use what exists now. Imperfect tools used consistently beat perfect tools you never implement.

Three simple technological interventions that take less than an hour combined but can dramatically change your trajectory.

Technology won't save you. But it can help you save yourself.

And in recovery, every bit of help matters.
