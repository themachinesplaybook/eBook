Escaping the Social Pull: How to Break Away from the Pokie Crowd

I need to be honest with you about something I didn't fully understand until years after I quit: the hardest part of walking away from Pokies wasn't leaving the machines. It was leaving the people.

When you're a regular at a Pokie venue, you may feel that you develop genuine friendships.

These aren't shallow relationships‚Äîyou share stories, support each other through rough patches, celebrate wins together.

I knew people's family dramas, their work troubles, their dreams and disappointments.

They knew mine.

We were bonded by our shared experience.

The problem is, that shared experience was slowly destroying all of us.

Understanding the Social Trap

The social bonds formed in Pokie venues are real, but they're also toxic. It's like being part of a support group where everyone's supporting each other in staying sick.

You care about these people, and they care about you, but the foundation of your relationship is mutual self-destruction. Research published in the Journal of Gambling Studies found that problem gamblers with strong social networks centred around gambling venues were significantly less likely to quit or seek treatment than isolated gamblers. Think about that. Having friends made it harder to recover because those friendships were entangled with the addiction.

The venues know this.

They deliberately cultivate these communities because they're incredibly effective at retention. A study by the Australian Institute of Family Studies found that gaming venues actively train staff to build relationships with regular customers, learn their names and preferences, and create an atmosphere of belonging.

This isn't accidental hospitality‚Äîit's strategic customer retention.

Defensive Strategies to Break Free

So how do you escape a social trap? How do you walk away from real relationships and genuine connection? Here are six strategies that helped me and others I've known:

Strategy 1: Recognise the Relationship Foundation

The first step is honest assessment. Ask yourself: would these friendships exist outside the venue? If the machines disappeared tomorrow, would you still spend time with these people? If you stopped gambling, would they still want to be around you? For most of my Pokie friendships, the answer was no.

We had nothing in common except gambling.

We never met for coffee elsewhere.

We didn't catch up for dinner or watch footy together. Our entire relationship was built around sitting next to each other feeding machines.

That doesn't mean the feelings weren't real.

They were (at least I thought so at the time).

But the foundation was rotten. Write down the names of your Pokie friends. Then write down the last time you interacted with them outside the venue.

If the page is mostly blank, you know what you're losing when you walk away‚Äînot genuine friendship, but the illusion of it.

Strategy 2: Find Your Third Place Elsewhere

Sociologist Ray Oldenburg coined the term 'third place' to describe social environments separate from home and work‚Äîplaces where people gather informally to connect and belong. The Pokie lounge was my third place. When I decided to quit, I had to find a new one.

This is crucial: you can't leave a void. Human beings need social connection.

If you remove the Pokie lounge without replacing it, you'll feel isolated and lonely, and the pull to return will be overwhelming.

For me, it was a local gym.

I started going to the same classes at the same times, saw the same faces, gradually built new friendships.

For others it might be a community garden, a volunteer organisation, a sports club, a hobby group, a church or community centre.

The key is finding a third place where the social bonds are built around something healthy.

Where spending time there improves your life instead of destroying it.

Where the community supports your growth instead of your addiction.

Strategy 3: Prepare for Social Pressure

If you announce that you're quitting gambling, some of your Pokie friends will actively try to pull you back in.

This isn't necessarily malicious‚Äîoften they're uncomfortable with your decision because it forces them to examine their own behaviour.

When someone quits, it breaks the normalisation. Suddenly the group has to face the reality that what they're doing might be harmful. It's easier to convince the person who quit to come back than to face that uncomfortable truth. Prepare your responses in advance:

  • 'I appreciate the offer, but I'm done with gambling.'
  • 'My finances need a break‚ÄîI'm focusing on other things.'
  • 'I've realised it's not good for me.

I need to stay away.'

  • 'I'll catch up with you outside the venue sometime, but I can't go back there.' practise saying these out loud.

When the pressure comes‚Äîand it will‚Äîyou need to have your responses ready. Don't debate or justify. State your position firmly and change the subject.

Strategy 4: Gradually Transition Relationships

For some of your Pokie friends, there might be potential for genuine friendship outside the venue.

But you won't know until you try to shift the relationship's foundation. Invite them to meet somewhere else. 'Hey, want to grab lunch next Tuesday instead of meeting at the venue?' If they agree and show up, that's a friendship worth preserving.

If they make excuses or only want to meet at the venue, you have your answer.

I had about a dozen people I considered friends from my Pokie days.

I invited all of them to meet elsewhere‚Äîcoffee, lunch, watching footy at a pub without machines. Three of them took me up on it. Only one of those relationships lasted beyond a few meetings.

That hurt. It felt like I'd lost eleven friendships.

But the truth is, I'd only ever had one real friend in that group.

The rest were gambling buddies who happened to share personal stories between spins.

Strategy 5: Build Accountability Outside the Venue

One of the reasons Pokie communities are so strong is the accountability they create. People expect to see you.

They notice if you're not there.

They ask where you've been.

This creates social pressure to attend, even when you don't want to.

You need to create competing accountability. Join a weekly commitment that happens at the same time you used to gamble. Book recurring appointments. Tell people outside the gambling world about your decision to quit and ask them to check in with you.

I joined an amateur 8-ball pool league that played Thursday nights because Thursday was my biggest gambling night. Instead of being at the venue from 6-10pm, I was at the pool hall competing in league matches. My teammates didn't know about my gambling history‚Äîthey knew I never missed a Thursday. The game gave me the competitive edge I craved, the gaming element I enjoyed, but without the financial harm.

That consistent commitment gave me accountability in a healthy direction.

Strategy 6: Grieve the Loss

Here's something people don't talk about enough: you're allowed to grieve when you leave the Pokie community. Even though the relationships were toxic and built on mutual destruction, they were still real to you. You're losing something that mattered. Don't minimise that loss. Don't tell yourself 'they weren't real friends anyway' and expect to feel nothing. You're losing a community, an identity, a place where you felt known and valued. That's a real loss, and it deserves to be mourned.

But grief has to have boundaries. Set aside specific time to feel it‚Äîmaybe 15 minutes each evening where you acknowledge the loss and let yourself feel sad about it. Then close that chapter and move forward. Grief becomes dangerous when it's constant and overwhelming, pulling you back to what you've left. Contained grief is healthy. Consuming grief is a relapse risk.

The Reality of Venue Staff

I want to address something specific: the staff who knew your name, brought your drink, asked about your day‚Äîthey weren't your friends.

I know that's harsh.

I know it felt like friendship.

But it wasn't.

Those staff members are trained to create that feeling. It's their job. In some venues, staff performance is literally measured by customer retention. They're taught your name and your drink order because it keeps you coming back and spending money. I'm not saying they're bad people or that their kindness was entirely fake.

Many of them probably did feel some genuine warmth toward regulars.

But the foundation of that relationship was transactional.

You were a customer.

They were providing a service.

When you stop being a customer, that relationship ends. Test it if you don't believe me. Stop gambling but visit the venue occasionally to say hello to the staff who 'cared' about you. See how long that warmth lasts when you're not feeding money into their machines.

Most times, it fades fast.

Breaking the Shared Misery Bond

One of the strangest elements of Pokie communities is the way shared loss creates bonding.

You commiserate about bad luck, near misses, losing streaks.

You share your worst gambling stories like war veterans sharing combat trauma. There's connection in that shared suffering.

But it's a toxic connection. It normalises destructive behaviour and frames it as something inevitable and shared rather than something you could choose to stop.

When you leave, you'll miss that shared understanding. You'll be around people who don't understand why you spent three hours yesterday feeding $200 into a machine.

They can't relate to the specific frustration of a near-miss or the hollow high of a small win on a big loss. That's a good thing.

You don't want to be around people who understand that experience because understanding often means they're living it too.

You want to be around people who think that behaviour sounds incomprehensible and concerning‚Äîbecause it is.

The Path Forward

Breaking free from the social bonds of Pokie venues is genuinely difficult. These are real relationships, even if they're unhealthy. You'll feel isolated initially. You'll miss the belonging, the familiar faces, the place where everybody knows your name.

But here's what I learned: that belonging came at a cost I couldn't afford to keep paying.

Every night I spent in that community was a night I wasn't building healthier relationships.

Every dollar I spent to maintain my status as a regular was a dollar I couldn't spend on building a life worth living.

The community you find outside the venue‚Äîbuilt on shared interests instead of shared destruction, on mutual growth instead of mutual loss‚Äîwill be stronger and more genuine. It takes time to build. It requires effort and vulnerability.

But it's worth it.

You deserve friendships that don't require you to destroy yourself to maintain them.

You deserve a third place where belonging makes your life better, not worse.

You deserve a community that celebrates your wins without requiring you to keep losing.

Those communities exist.

But you won't find them while you're still trapped in the Pokie lounge, surrounded by people who are trapped there with you, all of you pretending that this is fine, that you're all having a bit of fun, that the mounting losses and broken lives and financial devastation are the price of admission to this warm, welcoming, friendly little community. It's not fine. It never was.

And walking away from it‚Äîeven though it hurts, even though you'll miss people, even though you'll feel alone for a while‚Äîis the first step toward finding genuine belonging.

The machines want to isolate you with the illusion of community. Real freedom means building actual connections outside their reach.
